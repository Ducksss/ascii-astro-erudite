Getting into OCS did not feel like arriving at something I had always expected. Honestly, I did not think I was the obvious person for it.

## I always aimed for the best, but I still did not expect OCS

That may sound strange in hindsight, but it is the truth. I have always aimed for the best. That part of me did not disappear in SAF. I wanted to do well, push myself, and see how far I could go. But aiming for the best and expecting OCS were not the same thing for me.

I did not walk out of BMT thinking, of course I am heading to OCS. I was ambitious, yes, but I was also realistic about how uncertain I felt. So when I got in, part of my reaction was not triumph. It was surprise, maybe even a little confusion.

I had actually been hoping to get into DIS. In my head, that path made more intuitive sense. I came from a more technical background, and I thought that was where I would fit best. Somehow, I could not go there because I was too physically fit, which is still a funny sentence for me to say out loud.

That made the OCS outcome feel even stranger. I still do not think of that moment as a simple confirmation that I had everything figured out. If anything, it put me face to face with a question I had been able to avoid before: if I am here now, can I actually grow into what this requires?

## OCS made the pressure more serious

One of the memories that still makes me laugh a bit now is from the very first day. I vividly remember greeting the instructor who would be with me for the next three months as "sergeant," even though she was a captain. I simply did not know how to read ranks properly yet.

That moment captures a lot of how OCS felt at the start. I had made it in, but I still felt embarrassingly unfamiliar with the world I had stepped into. It was a very quick reminder that getting selected and actually being ready were two very different things.

In BMT, the struggle had been more immediate. Can I endure this? Can I keep up? Can I take one more day of this pace? OCS added a different question on top of all that: can I carry responsibility well?

That question made everything more serious. The standards were higher, the expectations sharper, and the margin for hiding much smaller. It was no longer enough to just survive things personally. I had to think more clearly, act more deliberately, and hold myself to a standard that other people could trust.

What I remember most is the way OCS exposed the gap between liking the idea of leadership and actually being prepared for it. Leadership sounds admirable from a distance. It feels noble, disciplined, even inspiring. But up close, leadership means pressure. It means accountability. It means that your decisions do not end with you.

I did not enter OCS as someone who already felt fully formed. I was still learning, still adjusting, still carrying self-doubt from earlier stages of SAF. In that sense, OCS was humbling. It did not reward image. It demanded steadiness. It demanded that I function even when I was uncertain, that I take responsibility even when I did not yet feel naturally confident.

## Responsibility came before confidence

That was one of the most important shifts for me. I used to imagine that confidence came first, and that good performance would follow from it. OCS taught me something closer to the opposite. A lot of the time, confidence is built afterward. First you are forced to perform under pressure. Then, slowly, you realize you are becoming someone who can.

There is also a different kind of fatigue in OCS. It is not only physical or routine-based. It is the fatigue of being evaluated against a higher bar, of knowing that the expectations are no longer about your own development alone. The pressure feels more serious because the consequences feel more serious. The environment starts shaping your sense of responsibility, not just your endurance.

## Signals Institute was fine, but strangely forgettable

After that, I went to Signals Institute.

It was alright, but if I am being honest, it was also kind of boring. There were CCNA lessons, and that was the main thing I remember from that period. Beyond that, there is really not much that stayed with me in the same way BMT or OCS did.

Maybe that in itself says something. Some phases of service mark you very deeply. Others just pass through you. Signals Institute was one of those quieter stretches for me. It was not bad. It just did not feel especially vivid or meaningful compared with the parts of SAF that came before and after it.

Looking back, I do not think of OCS as some huge dramatic turning point. It was okay. I learned what I needed to learn, got more used to the expectations, and moved through that phase of service the way I had to.

If anything, what stays with me is less some grand lesson and more the fact that I was still figuring things out while the system kept moving. I just learned along the way, and that was really it.
