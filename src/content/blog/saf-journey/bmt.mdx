BMT was rough for me in ways that are still easy to feel when I think back on that period.

## The term started feeling painfully long, very fast

It was not just the physical fatigue, though that was real. It was the total adjustment. Suddenly everything felt more rigid, more tiring, and less comfortable. Sleep never felt like enough. Small routines that I used to take for granted disappeared. Every day seemed to ask for energy that I was not sure I had.

That is the part people often compress too much when they tell military stories after the fact. They skip straight to the growth. They talk about discipline, resilience, and camaraderie, which are all real, but they leave out how hard the adjustment can be while you are actually inside it. For me, BMT did not begin as a heroic chapter. It began as a deeply uncomfortable one.

I did not feel strong most of the time. I felt tired. I felt stretched. I felt like I was learning to function in a place that had very little interest in whether I preferred the old version of my life. There is a particular kind of exhaustion that comes from being pushed physically while also trying to adapt mentally to a completely different environment, and BMT introduced me to that almost immediately.

What made it even harder was that there was no dramatic breakthrough at the start. I was not one of those people who immediately found meaning in the hardship or suddenly discovered hidden reserves of motivation. A lot of the time, I was simply trying to get through the next block, the next day, the next stretch of discomfort without mentally collapsing under it.

That honesty matters to me, because I think hardship only becomes useful if you are willing to admit that it really felt hard. BMT was where I learned what it meant to continue without feeling ready, capable, or inspired. That is a much less glamorous version of resilience than most people like to describe, but it is also a more truthful one.

More than once, the rest of my term just felt painful to think about. It was not only that the days were hard. It was that they felt endless when I was still so early in the journey. BMT gave me my first real taste of that feeling.

## The food was bad and I missed home badly

I also remember the mood of that period very clearly. The food was bad. I was melancholy for home far more than I expected. There is a particular kind of sadness that comes from being physically uncomfortable, emotionally out of place, and stuck inside a routine that you did not choose. BMT had a lot of that for me.

That homesickness made everything heavier. It was not just that I was tired. It was that I wanted familiar things back: familiar food, familiar spaces, familiar people, familiar freedom. When you are already being pushed, even small forms of longing become bigger than they should be.

## Meeting Nicholas made BMT more human

One of the biggest reasons BMT is not only a memory of pain for me is that it was where I met Nicholas.

He was my buddy, and that mattered more than I understood at the time. In an environment where everything feels stripped down and every day asks a lot from you, one steady person beside you changes the texture of the experience. BMT is still hard, but it stops feeling like you are carrying the whole thing alone.

If you are reading this, buddy, thanks for looking out for me.

The truth is, I was kind of a massive fuck-up at times, and he was still patient with me. That patience stayed with me. When I look back on BMT, I do not only remember the institution or the pressure. I remember that one person beside me could make the whole thing feel more survivable.

I remember that part clearly because military stories can become too abstract if you only describe systems, standards, and suffering. The people matter. Nicholas mattered. Having someone to go through the same stretch with made the difficult days more bearable and gave that chapter a human center, not just a disciplinary one.

After we POP-ed, our paths split. Nicholas eventually went to the Air Force, while I continued on a different route. That is part of what makes BMT friendships feel distinct in memory. You go through something intense together, and then life branches out again. But even when the postings change, some people stay tied to that chapter permanently in your mind.

## Somehow, the SIT test was where I came alive

What makes BMT funny in hindsight is that the parts I expected to hate were not always the parts I actually hated most.

I killed the SIT test.

For some reason, that environment clicked with me. I was giving my food away, genuinely excited to "camp," and weirdly energized by having problems to solve with the guys around me. Even the heat rash could not fully cancel that out. There was something fun about being outdoors, working through puzzles, and figuring things out together in a more immediate, practical way.

That contrast still amuses me. Day-to-day BMT could make me miserable, homesick, and annoyed with myself, but the moment things started feeling like a rough, sweaty puzzle with other people, I lit up. Maybe that says something about me. Even in a very different environment, I still liked problem-solving. I just happened to find it in mud, sweat, and heat instead of behind a keyboard.

## By POP, I felt more relief than triumph

In a strange way, BMT made me feel smaller before it made me feel stronger. It stripped away the version of myself that expected control, comfort, or confidence on demand. It showed me how quickly routines can disappear, how fragile convenience really is, and how much of daily life depends on invisible comforts that we only notice once they are gone.

But somewhere inside that discomfort, something did begin to change. Not in a loud way. Not in a cinematic montage way. More quietly than that. I started learning that I could keep moving even on days when I felt frustrated, worn down, and unsure of myself. I started learning that endurance does not always look impressive from the inside. Sometimes it just looks like refusing to give up on a bad day.

By the time POP came, I do not think my dominant feeling was pride. It was relief. Relief that I had made it through. Relief that the hardest early stretch had an ending. Relief that something I once doubted I could handle was now behind me.

That may be the most important thing BMT gave me. It did not make me feel exceptional. It taught me that resilience begins much closer to the ground. It begins with carrying discomfort without running from it. It begins with staying in the process long enough for it to shape you.
